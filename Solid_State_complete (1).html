Structural insight: The diamond cubic unit cell contains 8 carbon atoms — 4 from the FCC sublattice (corners ⅛ × 8 + face centres ½ × 6 = 4) and 4 from the alternating tetrahedral void sites. □ Schottky Defect
Missing Ion Pairs
Equal numbers of cations and anions leave their lattice sites and migrate to the crystal surface. The lattice shrinks but stoichiometry is preserved. Common in ionic crystals with similar ion sizes (NaCl, KCl, KBr, CsCl).
TypeVacancy defect
StoichiometryMaintained
DensityDecreases
Electrical conductivityIncreases (ion hopping)
Common examplesNaCl, KCl, AgBr, CsCl
Favoured byHigh temp, similar radii
NCERT Key Point
Schottky defect lowers the density of the crystal because some lattice sites become vacant. The number of missing cations equals the number of missing anions — electrical neutrality is preserved.

Ion in Interstitial Site
A smaller ion (usually cation) leaves its lattice site and occupies a nearby interstitial void, creating a vacancy–interstitial pair. Common when cation is much smaller than anion (AgCl, AgBr, ZnS).

Frenkel defect does not change density — the displaced ion remains in the crystal. AgBr shows both Schottky and Frenkel defects, used in photographic films.

Effect of Defects on Density
■ Schottky: \(Z\) decreases (vacancies) \(\rightarrow\) \(\rho\) decreases
■ Frenkel: \(Z\) unchanged (ion stays inside) \(\rightarrow\) \(\rho\) unchanged
■ Metal excess: \(Z\) increases slightly \(\rightarrow\) \(\rho\) slightly increases
■ Metal deficiency: \(Z\) decreases \(\rightarrow\) \(\rho\) decreases
□ Stoichiometric Defects
Schottky Defect
Equal missing ions; density ↓
Equal number of cation and anion vacancies. Stoichiometry maintained. Density decreases. Conductivity ↑ by ion hopping. Favoured when \(r^+ \approx r^-\) (similar radii). Examples: NaCl, KCl, KBr, CsCl, AgBr.
💡 Mnemonic: "Schottky Shrinks density" — ions leave crystal surface, leaving holes behind.
Frenkel Defect
Ion displaced to interstitial; density unchanged
Smaller ion (usually cation) jumps to interstitial void, leaving lattice vacancy. Density unchanged. Favoured when \(r^+ \ll r^-\). Examples: AgCl, AgBr, AgI, ZnS.
💡 Mnemonic: "Frenkel Finds a new home" — cation moves to interstitial but never leaves.
AgBr — special case
Shows both Schottky AND Frenkel
AgBr exhibits both defects simultaneously — used in photographic films. High ionic conductivity makes it photoresponsive.
+ Non-stoichiometric Defects
Metal Excess — Type 1 (F-centre)
Anion vacancy + trapped \(e^-\) → colour
Crystal heated in metal vapour → anions diffuse out → electrons trapped in anion vacancies (F-centres). Absorb visible light → colour.
\(\mathrm{NaCl} + \mathrm{Na}\text{ vapour} \rightarrow \mathrm{Na}^+\text{(lattice)} + e^-\text{(F-centre)} \rightarrow\) yellow
\(\mathrm{KCl} + \mathrm{K}\text{ vapour} \rightarrow \mathrm{K}^+\text{(lattice)} + e^-\text{(F-centre)} \rightarrow\) violet
Metal Excess — Type 2 (Interstitial cation)
Extra cation + extra electron
Extra cation in interstitial, charge balanced by free electron. Example: ZnO at high temperature becomes \(\mathrm{Zn}_{1+x}\mathrm{O}\) (white→yellow, n-type semiconductor).
Metal Deficiency
Missing cation; higher-charge ion compensates
Metal : non-metal < 1. Some \(\mathrm{M}^{2+}\) replaced by \(\mathrm{M}^{3+}\). Positive holes → p-type semiconductor. Examples: FeO, NiO, CrO.
💡 Mnemonic: "Deficiency → p-type" — fewer metals = holes = positive carriers.
